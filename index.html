Vegetables

The Facts About Vegetables

Asparagus

It takes three years from seed to harvest. Asparagus plants exhibit sexual differentiation.

Beet Greens

Cultivated since prehistoric times, early Romans only ate the beet tops, leaving the beet roots for medicinal purposes..

Beets

The beet was initially cultivated around 2,000 BC in the Mediterranean region, which makes it a natural fit for a heart-healthy Mediterranean diet.

Bell Peppers

The bell pepper is the only member of the pepper family that does not produce capsaicin. The absence of capsaicin is because of a recessive form of a gene that gets rid of capsaicin. That’s also why it gets the name of sweet peppers!

Bok Choy

Bok choy might look a lot like celery, but it’s a member of the cabbage family.

Broccoli

Broccoli by invented by crossing cauliflower seeds with pea seeds.

Brussels sprouts

Brussels sprouts may stink when you cook them because they are especially rich in nutrients known as glucosinolates, which contain sulfur.

Cabbage

Cabbage is one of the oldest vegetables in existence and continues to be a dietary staple throughout the world.

Carrots

People first grew carrots as medicine, not food, for a variety of ailments.

Cauliflower

Cauliflower comes in 4 different colors-Purple, Green, Orange, and White.

Celery

Cultivation of celery is believed to have started 3000 years ago in the Mediterranean region. Celery was first used as a food during the 16th century in Italy.

Collard Greens

Collard greens date back to prehistoric times, and are one of the oldest members of the cabbage family.

Sweet Corn

Sweet corn leaves were used as chewing gum by Native Americans. On average there are about 800 kernels on an ear of corn.

Cucumbers

Next time you're feeling dehydrated and don't feel like drinking water, try snacking on a cucumber; it's 96 percent water.

Eggplant

Eggplant or aubergine is a species of nightshade grown for its edible fruit.

Fennel

Fennel is a flowering plant species in the carrot family. It is grown for its edible bulbs, shoots, leaves, and seeds.

Garlic

The sticky juice that’s in garlic cloves is often used as an adhesive, especially for delicate projects that involve fragile items like glass.

Green Beans

Raw green beans are actually a little bit toxic. There are two types of green beans - Pole beans and Bush beans.

Green Peas

The green pea as we know it today is only green because it’s picked when it’s still immature. A ripe pea is yellowish.

Kale

After a frost, kale becomes sweeter. The taste of kale can be surprisingly varied, from bitter or peppery, to more plain and slightly sweet.

Leeks

The Greek philosopher Aristotle credited the clear voice of the partridge to a diet of leeks, while the Roman emperor Nero supposedly ate leeks everyday to make his voice stronger..

Crimini Mushrooms

The entire crimini mushroom is edible, unlike the mature Portobello with its inedible stem.

Shiitake Mushrooms

The word “shiitake” is derived from the Japanese words "shii," the oak-like tree where these mushrooms first grew, and "také," which simply means mushroom; hence, it literally means “mushrooms of the shii tree.”

Mustard Greens

Mustard greens are the most pungent of the cooking greens and lend a peppery flavor to food. They originated in the Himalayan region of India more than 5,000 years ago.

Olives

The olive is a fruit, not a vegetable. Can be green, purple, dark brown, black, and even pink color.

Onions

Eating parsley will help get rid of that pesky onion breath! Note to self.

Potatoes

Despite its appearance, potato is made up of 80% water and only 20% of solid.

Romaine Lettuce

Romaine lettuce, botanically classified as Lactuca sativa, is believed to be one of the oldest varieties of lettuce in the world and is a member of the Asteraceae family

Sea Vegetables

The types of sea vegetablesare Agar, Arame, Dulse, Hijiki, Kelp, Kombu, Nori and Wakame. Sea Vegetables are one of the most alkalizing foods on the planet.

Spinach

Raw spinach is 91% water. Celebrate this leafy green on March 26th which is National Spinach Day!

Summer Squash

Summer squash are squashes that are harvested when immature, while the rind is still tender and edible. They are Cousa squash, Pattypan squash Tromboncino or zucchetta, Crookneck squash, Straightneck squash, zucchini and aehobak.

Winter Squash

M&M's stands for Mars & Murrie, who created the famous candy.

Sweet Potatoes

Popsicles were invented by 11-year-old Frank Epperson when he left a mixture of powdered soda, water and a mixing stick out overnight in freezing temperatures.

Swiss Chard

Swiss chard is a biennial vegetable primarily grown for its edible leaves.

Tomatoes

There are over 10,000 varieties of tomato, these come in a variety of colours including pink, purple, black, yellow and white.

Turnip greens

Both the root and the leaves of the turnip are edible, but turnip greens refer specifically to the stem and leafy green part of the plant.
